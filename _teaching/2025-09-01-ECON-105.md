### Course Description
This 4-credit course introduces students to the U.S. and global economies through a **political-economy lens**. Economic activity is treated as embedded in social and institutional contexts that evolve historically. Core themes include capitalism’s structure, markets, the role of the state, inequality, unemployment, environmental limits, crisis and stagnation, and possible economic alternatives.

### Textbook
- Bowles, Edwards & Roosevelt (2018). *Understanding Capitalism: Competition, Command, and Change* (4th ed.)  
Additional readings from Basu, Dunn, Vidal et al., and Rochon & Rossi are provided on Canvas.

### Assignments
Short papers (≈ 2 pages) and online discussions align with weekly topics. Exams are essay-based and time-constrained, with questions released in advance.

### Academic Honesty
All work must be original. Any AI-assisted text must follow Cheng et al. (2025) ethical guidelines on transparency, review, verification, and accountability.

### Topics by Unit
1. Introduction — *Understanding Capitalism* chs. 1 & 3  
2. Human Nature & Behavior — ch. 2; Johnson; Harris  
3. Production & Surplus — ch. 4  
4. Markets — chs. 8–9  
5. Economic Systems — ch. 5 (89–96)  
6. Capitalism — ch. 5 (96–112), ch. 7, ch. 10 (207–220); Basu (2022)  
7. U.S. Economy — ch. 7 (137–141)  
8. Government & Economy — ch. 6; ch. 19 (516–527); Dunn (2014) ch. 6  
9. Global Economy — ch. 17 (435–447); Dunn (2014) chs. 7–9  
10. Environment — Boyce (2003, 2025); Moore (2022) or Frame (2022)  
11. Inequality — ch. 14  
12. Unemployment & Business Cycles — ch. 16; EPI (2024)  
13. Social Structures of Accumulation — ch. 7; Kotz (2009); Pagano (2014) or Cox (2019)  
14. Contemporary Capitalism — Durand & Milberg (2020); Rikap (2023); Morozov (2019); Muldoon (2025)
